Why This Matters: Evidence When You Need It

A dashcam only protects you if it's recording when an incident happens. Many drivers assume their dashcam has "enough" storage without understanding the math: a 128GB card at typical 1080p 30fps bitrate (12 Mbps) gives you roughly 10 days of continuous 24/7 recording. If you leave your car parked overnight and it gets hit, you need that 10 days of buffer to ensure the footage exists when police ask for evidence.

Dashcams write constantlyâ€”24/7 if parking mode is enabled. This brutal write pattern destroys standard SD cards within months, but high-endurance cards are specifically engineered to handle millions of write cycles. Using a standard card in a dashcam is false economy: it fails faster and may corrupt critical footage just when you need it.

This calculator ensures you buy the right card and understand your coverage window. Combined with endurance card recommendations, you'll have reliable evidence capture every time you drive.

Frequently Asked Questions

Loop Recording: Continuous circular buffer. Your dashcam records continuously. When the microSD card reaches capacity, the oldest footage automatically overwrites with new footage. This ensures you always have the most recent footage without manual card swaps.

How it protects you: If your 256GB card records for 25 days of continuous 24/7 footage, you always have the last 25 days archived. If an accident happens today, footage from 30 days ago is gone, but today's incident is captured. This rolling buffer is critical for legal protection.

Important consideration: If you have an accident or incident, immediately copy/backup that footage to a computer. Once the loop overwrites past that date, the footage is gone permanently. Many dashcams have "incident protection" that locks footage segments when impact is detected, preventing overwrite.

For storage planning: Our calculator shows loop duration in days. A longer buffer is saferâ€”aim for at least 14 days (covers two weeks of "emergency backup window") if possible. Most drivers are comfortable with 7-14 days.

High-Endurance Cards: Specialized for constant write operations. Examples: SanDisk Max Endurance, Samsung PRO Endurance. These cards have firmware optimizations and wear-leveling algorithms specifically designed for 24/7 recording.

Why standard cards fail: Standard microSD cards are rated for intermittent use (photography, casual video). They have limited write cycle budgets. Dashcam loop recording taxes this budget aggressively. A standard 256GB card might fail in 1-2 years of continuous operation. Endurance cards are engineered for 5+ years of 24/7 use.

The math: Standard card might handle 10 million write cycles. Continuous dashcam recording cycles through data constantly, exhausting budget quickly. Endurance cards handle 100+ million write cycles, explicitly rated for dashcam/surveillance workloads.

Cost consideration: Endurance cards cost 20-30% more, but last 5x longer. Over a 5-year car ownership, it's cheaper than replacing failed cards every year or two. Plus, a failed card mid-incident loses critical evidence.

For typical 1080p dashcam (12 Mbps / 1.5 MB/s): ~25 days of continuous 24/7 recording. This gives you a rolling 25-day archive.

For 2K dashcam (20 Mbps / 2.5 MB/s): ~15 days of continuous recording.

For 4K dashcam (40 Mbps / 5 MB/s): ~7-8 days of continuous recording.

Practical meaning: 256GB is the "sweet spot" for most dashcam users. 25 days gives a solid buffer (covers a full month of weekday driving plus weekends). If an accident happens, there's plenty of footage history to capture context leading up to the incident.

128GB (smaller vehicles, budget option): ~12 days of continuous 1080p recording. Good for daily commutes; covers a work week plus a few days. May be risky if you park for extended periods and want longer historical buffer.

256GB (recommended for most drivers): ~25 days of continuous 1080p recording. Covers a full month effectively. Safest balance of cost and protection. Our standard recommendation.

512GB (extended buffer or 4K recording): ~50 days for 1080p or 14+ days for 4K. Ideal if you want maximum protection, 4K quality, or rarely delete footage. Higher capacity = longer evidence window if incident investigation is delayed.

Our recommendation: Buy 256GB endurance card. It's the sweet spot: affordable, provides robust 25-day buffer, and excellent longevity. If your budget allows and you want peace of mind, upgrade to 512GB.

Incident Protection (built-in): Most modern dashcams have "incident detection" or "impact detection." When the camera detects sudden acceleration, deceleration, or impact, it locks that footage segment, preventing overwrites. Locked footage stays protected even when the loop continues. Check your dashcam's manual for this feature.

Manual Backup: If you witness an incident or suspect your dashcam captured important footage, immediately power off the dashcam, remove the card, and back up footage to a computer. Don't waitâ€”the loop may overwrite before you remember. Keep the card removed until the incident is resolved.

Cloud Backup (if supported): Some newer dashcams offer WiFi cloud upload. Footage automatically uploads to cloud storage. This gives you off-device backup. Review your dashcam model's cloud capabilities.

Multi-Card Rotation (professional setup): Use multiple cards and rotate them manually or with the camera's quick-swap design. When one card is full, swap to a fresh card and back up the full one. Overkill for casual drivers, but common in commercial fleets.
