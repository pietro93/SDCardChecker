Why This Matters: Always-On Security

Surveillance footage is only useful if it's captured when incidents occur. A security camera that stops recording because its card filled or failed defeats the entire purpose of having security in the first place. Understanding your storage capacity and retention window is critical for effective surveillance.

Surveillance cards are under extreme stress: writing continuously 24/7 with no rest. Standard microSD cards degrade in 2-6 months under this workload. High-endurance cards are purpose-built to handle millions of write cycles and last 3-5 years. Using a standard card is false economyâ€”it fails exactly when you need it most.

This calculator ensures your coverage is continuous and reliable. Know your retention window, invest in endurance cards, and monitor system health regularly.

Surveillance Storage Guide

Loop Recording: Surveillance systems record continuously into a circular buffer. When the microSD card reaches capacity, the oldest footage automatically overwrites with new recording. This ensures continuous coverage without manual intervention.

Retention Window: The time span of footage you can review. A 256GB card recording 1080p 24/7 at 2.5 Mbps gives ~25 days. You always have the last 25 days; footage older than that is permanently gone (overwritten).

For evidence: If you need footage from 30 days ago, a 25-day retention isn't enough. Consider larger-capacity cards or backup systems. Most security professionals recommend 30-60 day retention minimum for important installations.

Continuous stress: Surveillance writes 24/7 non-stop. Standard microSD cards are rated for intermittent use and fail within months. Endurance cards have specialized firmware for continuous operation.

Lifespan difference: Standard card: 2-6 months before failure. Endurance card: 3-5 years of continuous operation. The cost difference (20-30% premium) pays for itself through reduced replacement frequency and critical uptime.

Examples: SanDisk Max Endurance, Samsung PRO Endurance are industry standards for surveillance. They carry extended warranties and are explicitly rated for 24/7 recording.

1080p 24/7 at 2.5 Mbps: ~25 days retention window

2K 24/7 at 3 Mbps: ~20 days retention window

4K 24/7 at 6 Mbps: ~10 days retention window

For most home/small business: 256GB provides 15-25 days coverage depending on resolution. Adequate for personal security. For critical installations, 512GB-1TB is recommended for 30-60+ day retention.

Home security (1-2 cameras): 256GB endurance card. Provides 2+ weeks retention, adequate for most incidents.

Business/Multi-camera (3-8 cameras): 512GB-1TB per camera, or larger centralized storage. Ensures 30+ day retention for each camera, critical for incident investigation and liability.

Critical installations: Multiple cards per camera, redundant backup systems. Never rely on single card for critical security.

Local backup: Periodically (weekly/monthly) copy entire card contents to external hard drive or NAS. If card fails, you have historical backup.

Cloud backup: Some surveillance systems support cloud upload. Provides off-site redundancy but may have bandwidth/cost limitations.

Multi-card rotation: Use multiple cards and rotate manually. When one fills, back it up and reformat. Ensures multiple generations of footage exist.

Best practice: Local + cloud backup for critical installations. At minimum, weekly local backup to external drive.

Warning signs: Recording stops unexpectedly. Video quality degrades. Camera reboots frequently. Corrupted footage. These indicate imminent card failure.

Prevention: Use endurance cards explicitly. Replace cards every 2-3 years even if they seem fine (wear-out is inevitable). Monitor system logs for errors.

Replacement schedule: Standard cards: every 6-12 months. Endurance cards: every 3-5 years. Budgeting for periodic replacement is essential for reliable surveillance.
