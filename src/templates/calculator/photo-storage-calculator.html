Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between JPEG and RAW?

JPEG: Compressed format. Smaller files (2‚Äì12MB per photo). Ready to share, but limited editing flexibility. Processing happens in-camera.

RAW: Uncompressed format. Larger files (20‚Äì80MB per photo depending on megapixels). Maximum editing control ‚Äî you process in post-production with software like Lightroom or Capture One.

Best for beginners? JPEG. Best for professionals or serious enthusiasts? RAW.

How do I know my camera's file size?

Method 1: Shoot a test photo, check file properties (right-click ‚Üí Properties).

Method 2: Search "[Camera Model] RAW file size" ‚Äî reviews often list this.

Method 3: Use our presets above ‚Äî pick your megapixel range (5MP, 20MP, 45MP) and format.

What Speed Class do I need for burst shooting?

For JPEG bursts: Class 10 or U3 is usually fine. Low write speed needed.

For RAW bursts: V30 minimum. RAW is fast-writing, so faster cards prevent buffer slowdown.

For high-speed burst (10+ fps): V60 or better. Professional bursting requires consistent performance.

Can I mix JPEG and RAW on the same card?

Yes. Many cameras have a "JPEG + RAW" mode that saves both formats simultaneously. This uses roughly double the space ‚Äî calculate as if you're shooting both, or use RAW file size as the baseline (since JPEG is negligible next to RAW).

Is 256GB overkill for photos?

Not necessarily. RAW shooters taking 5,000+ photos per shoot can fill 256GB quickly. But most JPEG shooters are fine with 64GB. Use our calculator to see your own requirements based on your shooting style.

Should I shoot RAW or JPEG?

Shoot RAW if: You like post-processing, want maximum editing control, or shoot professionally.

Shoot JPEG if: You want instant, ready-to-share photos or are just starting out.

Best option? Many cameras let you shoot both (JPEG + RAW). Use JPEG for quick sharing and RAW for serious editing.
