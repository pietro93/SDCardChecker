Why This Matters: Complex Projects Need Accurate Planning

Timelapse photography operates at a different scale than standard shooting. A 24-hour construction project shooting every 5 seconds produces 17,280 photos. Shooting RAW at 30MB per file means 518GB of storage. Miss this calculation and your card fills at hour 6, leaving 18 hours unrecorded.

The relationship between shooting interval and final video length adds another layer. Those same 17,280 photos at 24fps playback equal 12 minutes of video. Understanding this math before you start helps you plan both storage and content duration for client deliverables and creative projects.

Timelapse Shooting Guide

Basic formula: Shooting Interval (seconds) = (Project Duration in hours Ã— 3600) Ã· Desired Video Length in seconds Ã· Playback FPS

Example: 24-hour project, 30-second final video, 24fps playback = (24 Ã— 3600) Ã· 30 Ã· 24 = 120-second interval (2 minutes between photos).

Common intervals:

  • 2 seconds: Fast motion (clouds, sun movement, traffic). Dense photo count for smooth playback.
  • 5-10 seconds: Moderate motion (construction, weather changes). Balanced file size and smooth video.
  • 30 seconds: Slow motion (plant growth, sunrise/sunset). Very manageable file sizes for long projects.
  • 60+ seconds: Ultra-long projects (architectural time-lapses). Minimal storage, sparse but effective.

Speed rule of thumb: Slower interval = more photos = smoother video = larger files. Faster interval = fewer photos = choppier video = smaller files. Find your sweet spot.

At 2-second intervals (dense, smooth video):

  • JPEG (8MB avg): ~345GB
  • RAW (30MB avg): ~1.3TB

At 5-second intervals (moderate):

  • JPEG: ~138GB
  • RAW: ~518GB

At 10-second intervals:

  • JPEG: ~69GB
  • RAW: ~259GB

Practical takeaway: A 256GB card handles 24-hour timelapse at 5+ second intervals in JPEG, or 10+ second intervals in RAW. Larger intervals or multi-card systems needed for dense (2-second) RAW shooting.

JPEG (smaller files, faster workflow): Better for long-duration timelapses where storage is tight. Smaller file sizes fit more photos on cards. Acceptable for most timelapse contentâ€”the final video is compressed anyway. In-camera color grading already applied.

RAW (maximum editing control, large files): Ideal for high-value projects where final quality is critical. Allows post-processing color correction, exposure compensation across the sequence. Worth the extra storage if you're doing professional work.

Hybrid approach: Some cameras allow JPEG+RAW mode. Capture JPEG for quick editing reference, RAW for final quality. Uses roughly 2x storage but gives maximum flexibility.

Recommendation: For casual/documentary timelapse, JPEG is fine. For professional or creative work, RAW. Use our calculator to estimate file sizes and decide based on your project budget and storage availability.

General tips: (1) Use fully charged battery (extended interval shooting draws significant power). (2) Format card in camera before starting. (3) Set focus to manual and lock focus distance (autofocus uses more power). (4) Use manual exposure (M-mode) so lighting doesn't change between shots. (5) Disable image stabilization (unnecessary for stationary tripod). (6) Monitor first 10-20 shots to confirm focus and exposure are consistent.

Playback FPS: Frames per second at which your photos are played back as video. 24fps is cinema standard. 30fps is TV standard.

How it affects duration: Same photo count at different fps creates different video lengths. 1000 photos at 24fps = 41.7 seconds. At 30fps = 33.3 seconds.

How it affects motion smoothness: Higher fps (30, 60) = smoother motion. Lower fps (24, 12) = more jerky motion. Most timelapse uses 24fps as standard. Some creators intentionally use 12fps for artistic choppiness.

For editing: Export your sequence as 24fps video unless your project requires otherwise. Most online platforms and delivery specs assume 24fps.

Adobe Lightroom (easiest): Import all photos. Select entire sequence. File â†’ Create Slideshow Video. Choose fps, resolution, export format. Lightroom handles sorting and sequencing automatically.

Adobe Premiere Pro (professional): Import sequence of images as continuous footage. Premiere auto-sequences based on filename numbering. Adjust fps in timeline settings. Export to desired format/codec.

DaVinci Resolve (powerful, free): Import image sequence. Create new timeline with desired fps. Drag images to timeline. Resolve auto-sequences. Color grade if needed. Export video.

FFmpeg (command-line, fast): For advanced users. Can batch-process large sequences quickly without GUI overhead.

General workflow: (1) Import photos in sequence order (ensure filenames are numbered consistently). (2) Set playback fps (24 is standard). (3) Color grade/correct if needed. (4) Export to H.264 or H.265 codec for compatibility. (5) Add music/sound in final step.
