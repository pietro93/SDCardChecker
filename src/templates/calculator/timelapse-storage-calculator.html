Why This Matters: The Challenge of Long-Duration Timelapse

Timelapse photography can reveal the beauty in time itselfâ€”clouds drifting across a landscape, stars wheeling overhead, a building rising from foundation to completion. But the math is unforgiving. Long timelapse projects write constantly to the SD card, which can degrade performance or cause failure if the card overheats or fills. Here are essential practices:

  • Use fast, high-capacity cards: V30 minimum, V60 recommended. A fast card handles sustained writes without throttling. Larger cards (128GB+) distribute write operations, reducing stress.
  • Multiple cards: For projects exceeding 50,000 photos, use multiple cards. Format one, swap it when full, and continue on the next. Safer than relying on a single card.
  • Keep the card cool: Sustained writing generates heat. In hot climates, consider shading your camera or using a fan. Card failure is more likely when cards overheat.
  • Format in-camera: Always format cards in your camera before starting. Formatting on your computer can cause compatibility issues.
  • Avoid full cards: If your card approaches 100% capacity, stop and swap. A completely full card risks corrupting the last few frames or crashing your camera.

Using our calculator, you can plan your shoot to ensure no single card exceeds 80% capacity, giving you a safety margin.
