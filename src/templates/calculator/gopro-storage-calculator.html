Short answer: V30 is typically sufficient for Hero 13 4K 120fps (150 Mbps = 18.75 MB/s), but V60 is recommended for margin of safety.

Why the gap? GoPro officially recommends U3 or V30 for most recording modes. However, 4K 120fps creates sustained writes at the high end of V30's capability. A V60 card has double the headroom (60 MB/s vs 30 MB/s), preventing buffer issues if your card isn't quite at peak performance or if temperatures fluctuate.

Practical guidance: V30 microSD cards work and are cheaper. If you're on a budget and stick to 4K 120fps (not higher bitrate modes), V30 is okay. If you want absolute reliability for extended recording or 5.3K modes, upgrade to V60. Professional content creators use V60+ to eliminate any risk of dropped frames.

Pro tip: Always buy from reputable brands (SanDisk, Samsung, Lexar). Counterfeit cards are common in the microSD market and don't deliver promised speed classes. A fake V60 at V30 actual speed causes exactly the problems you're trying to avoid.

Best practice: Always format your microSD card in the GoPro camera, not on a computer. GoPro formats to the exact filesystem and structure it expects, ensuring compatibility and performance.

Steps: (1) Insert new microSD card into GoPro. (2) Go to Settings > Formatting. (3) Select "Format Card" or similar (wording varies by model). (4) Confirm (this erases all data on the card). (5) Wait for completion (usually 30 seconds to 2 minutes depending on capacity).

When to reformat: Before first use on a new camera. Periodically during intensive shooting (weekly if recording 8+ hours per day) to prevent filesystem fragmentation. Never during active recording or low batteryâ€”always charge first.

Windows/Mac formatting: Avoid formatting in your computer. If you must (e.g., on a replacement/backup card), use exFAT format on Windows or Mac, then reformat in the GoPro. Computer-formatted cards sometimes cause compatibility quirks.
