JPEG: Compressed format. File sizes range from 2-8 MB depending on resolution and quality. JPEG is "locked in"‚Äîlimited editing flexibility. Great for casual photography, social media, quick sharing.

RAW: Uncompressed format. File sizes range from 30-65 MB depending on resolution. RAW contains maximum editing data: white balance, exposure, shadows, highlights all adjustable in post. Essential for professional work where control matters.

Storage impact: A 20MP camera: 8 MB per JPEG vs. 30 MB per RAW. On a 256 GB card: ~30,000 JPEGs vs. 8,000 RAWs.

Mixed shooting: Many professionals shoot JPEG + RAW simultaneously. Your card needs enough space for both‚Äîuse the calculator with combined file sizes.

Speed class depends on burst rate and file size:

  • V30: Minimum for most burst shooting (2-10 fps). Safe for JPEG bursts and smaller RAW files.
  • V60: For high-speed burst (20+ fps) or large RAW files. Professional cameras shooting 12+ fps bursts need V60.
  • V90: Overkill for photography. Mainly for video or extreme professional use.

Quick rule: Check your camera's burst specs. If it can shoot 10+ fps in RAW, use V60. Otherwise, V30 is fine.

Short answer: No. For professional work, bigger is better. Here's why:

  • Overprovision for reliability: Filling a card to 100% capacity reduces lifespan. Professionals keep cards 50-70% full to maximize durability.
  • Reduce card swaps: One 256 GB card beats three 64 GB cards. Fewer swaps = lower corruption risk and faster workflow.
  • Future-proof: Camera resolutions increase. What fits today might not fit your next camera.
  • Price-to-capacity ratio: A 256 GB card costs only 2-3x more than 64 GB, but holds 4x the data.

Pro recommendation: Buy cards larger than your immediate need. Use 50-70% of capacity, then archive to external drives.

Shoot RAW if: You do professional work, care about post-processing control, or want maximum editing flexibility. Examples: portraits, weddings, product photography.

Shoot JPEG if: You prioritize speed, storage efficiency, or don't edit photos much. Examples: social media, travel, casual events, real estate.

Shoot both (JPEG+RAW) if: You're a professional who wants instant JPEGs for client previews plus RAW for final editing. Storage is not an issue for professional work.

Storage rule of thumb: RAW requires 4-6 TB of storage per 10,000 photos. JPEG requires only 40-60 GB. If you shoot 10,000 photos per month, archive to external drives immediately.
