Why This Matters: The High Cost of Video Recording Failures

Dropped Frames & Corrupted Footage

Using the wrong card for your bitrate causes dropped frames and corrupted files. Your camera stops recording mid-shot, ruining takes. A $20 card savings isn't worth a lost wedding ceremony or event.

Buffer Overflow & Write Slowdown

Recording at 4K 60fps generates massive data streams. If your card's write speed is too slow, the camera's buffer fills up and recording pauses. You get gaps in your footage, stuttering, or automatic quality reduction.

Bitrate Miscalculation = Wrong Capacity

Many filmmakers guess their bitrate. Too conservative, and you waste money on oversized cards. Too aggressive, and you run out of storage mid-project. Knowing the exact number lets you buy the right capacity for the job.

Frequently Asked Questions

H.264 is the standard codec for most cameras and requires the most storage. A 4K H.264 stream at 30fps typically uses 100-150 Mbps.

H.265 (HEVC) is more efficient‚Äîit achieves the same quality at 50% lower bitrate. The same 4K 30fps stream uses only 50-80 Mbps. It's newer, so older devices may not support playback.

ProRes is a professional codec designed for editing. It uses higher bitrates (200-500 Mbps) but ensures maximum quality and compatibility with professional workflows. It's larger but worth it for commercial work.

Storage impact: H.264 4K 60fps = ~200 Mbps = 2.5 GB/min. H.265 4K 60fps = ~100 Mbps = 1.2 GB/min. ProRes 4K 60fps = ~500 Mbps = 3.75 GB/min.

Speed class determines minimum sustained write speed. Here's what you need:

  • V30: 30 MB/s write speed. For standard 4K (100-150 Mbps). This covers most consumer cameras.
  • V60: 60 MB/s write speed. For high-frame-rate 4K (4K 60fps, 4K 120fps, professional codecs).
  • V90: 90 MB/s write speed. For 8K, RAW video, or streaming. Overkill for most 4K scenarios.

Quick rule: Calculate your bitrate in Mbps. If it's under 90 Mbps, V30 is sufficient. Between 90-200 Mbps, use V60. Over 200 Mbps, use V90.

Three methods to find your camera's bitrate:

Method 1: Check Your Manual
The easiest approach. Search "[Camera Model] bitrate specifications" or check your manual's technical specs section.

Method 2: Record a Test File
Record 1 minute of video at your chosen resolution/fps. Check file size (MB). Multiply by 8 to get Mbps. Example: 180 MB file = 1,440 Mbps for 60 seconds = 24 Mbps.

Method 3: Use Common Benchmarks
‚Ä¢ 1080p 30fps: ~50 Mbps
‚Ä¢ 1080p 60fps: ~100 Mbps
‚Ä¢ 4K 30fps: ~100-150 Mbps
‚Ä¢ 4K 60fps: ~200-300 Mbps
These vary by codec (H.264 is higher, H.265 is lower).

Most cameras use full-size SD cards, but some (action cameras, drones, smartphones) use microSD. Always check your camera's manual before buying.

Key difference: MicroSD cards are physically smaller. Performance specs (speed class, write speed) are the same, but they have smaller capacity options (max ~512 GB vs. 1 TB for full-size SD).

If you need full-size SD and only have microSD cards, you can use an adapter‚Äîbut this doesn't improve the card's performance, only allows physical compatibility.

UHS (Ultra High Speed) is the interface standard for fast data transfer:

  • UHS-I: Max 104 MB/s. Older standard, still sufficient for most 4K recording.
  • UHS-II: Max 312 MB/s. Modern standard. Better for high-bitrate 4K 60fps and professional work.
  • UHS-III: Max 624 MB/s. Newest, for next-gen cameras. Overkill for current 4K scenarios.

Does it matter? Check your camera's specs. If your camera supports UHS-II, use UHS-II cards for faster offload. But for recording stability, speed class matters more than UHS version.

Even though your calculator says "you need 50 GB," the camera uses some space for metadata, file system structures, and firmware. This is overhead.

Typical overhead: 5-15%. A 256 GB card only has ~237 GB usable for video. We recommend 10% buffer by default.

Why it matters: If you calculate exactly 100 GB needed and buy a 100 GB card, you'll only fit ~90 GB of video. You'd need a 128 GB card instead.

Pro tip: Add extra buffer (15-25%) for safety. It's cheap insurance against dropped frames if the card gets fragmented over time.
