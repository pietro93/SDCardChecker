UHS Speed Class (U-Class)

The UHS Speed Class guarantees minimum sustained write speeds for video recording. It's the most commonly used speed rating today.

U1 Speed Class

Minimum Write Speed: 10 MB/s

The most basic UHS speed class, suitable for most recording needs.

Recommended for:

  • ✓ Basic video recording on smartphones
  • ✓ Standard photography
  • ✓ Everyday casual recording

Not suitable for:

  • ✗ 4K video recording
  • ✗ High frame rate recording
  • ✗ Action cameras

U3 Speed Class

Minimum Write Speed: 30 MB/s

The highest UHS speed class, recommended for demanding video and photography tasks.

Recommended for:

  • ✓ 4K video recording (30fps)
  • ✓ Action cameras (GoPro, DJI)
  • ✓ Drones and UAVs
  • ✓ High-speed photography
  • ✓ Gaming consoles (Steam Deck, Switch)

May not be ideal for:

  • ⚠ 8K video recording
  • ⚠ High frame rate 4K (120fps+)
  • ⚠ Professional cinema work

Video Speed Class (V-Class)

Video Speed Classes specifically measure sustained write speeds during video recording. They're more precise than U-Class for video applications.

V6

6 MB/s minimum

Entry-level video. Suitable for basic video recording but lacks headroom for professional work.

V30

30 MB/s minimum

Professional 4K video. Industry standard for professional videography and cinema.

V60

60 MB/s minimum

High-bitrate 4K and 8K video. Required for demanding cinema and professional productions.

V90

90 MB/s minimum

Highest class. For cutting-edge 8K cinema and extreme performance demands.

Bus Speed (UHS Interface)

Bus speed indicates the maximum theoretical transfer rate between the card and device. This is different from speed classes.

I

UHS-I Bus

Maximum: 104 MB/s

Legacy bus interface. Found in older devices. Still functional but slower than newer standards.

II

UHS-II Bus

Maximum: 312 MB/s

Commonly used in professional cameras and high-performance cards. Provides significant speed improvement over UHS-I.

III

UHS-III Bus

Maximum: 624 MB/s

Newest standard for professional equipment. Enables very fast data transfers for demanding workflows.

Speed Class Guide by Use Case

Photography

Recommended: U1 to U3

For still photography, even high-speed burst shooting, U1 is usually sufficient. Use U3 if you shoot RAW + JPEG or need faster card reader speeds.

4K Video (30fps)

Recommended: U3 or V30

Essential for continuous 4K recording. U3/V30 ensures smooth, uninterrupted 4K video capture at standard frame rates. Both are equivalent for this use case.

4K Video (60fps+)

Recommended: V60 or higher

High frame rate 4K requires sustained speeds of 60 MB/s or more. Look for V60 or V90 rated cards.

Drones & Action Cameras

Recommended: U3 (V30 if available)

Most consumer drones and action cameras need U3 minimum. Check your specific device requirements, but U3 covers most scenarios.

Gaming Consoles

Recommended: U3

Nintendo Switch, Steam Deck, and other gaming devices benefit from U3 cards for faster game loading and install times.

Smartphones & Tablets

Recommended: U1 (microSD)

Most phones and tablets use microSD cards. U1 is sufficient for most users, though U3 is better for 4K video recording on phones.

Professional Cinema

Recommended: V60 to V90

Professional video production demands the highest speed classes. V60 is industry standard for cinema cameras, V90 for demanding workflows.

Common Questions

Is a faster speed class always better?

Not necessarily. A faster card is only beneficial if your device can use those speeds. However, having extra headroom is never a bad thing and ensures compatibility with future devices.

Can I use a faster card in a device that needs a slower class?

Yes. Speed classes are minimum guarantees, not maximums. A U3 card works perfectly fine in a device that needs U1. You just won't see the speed benefit.

What's the difference between U-Class and V-Class?

V-Class is more specific to video recording and has tighter specifications. For most purposes, U3 and V30 are equivalent. V-Class is better for professional video work.

Does bus speed matter more than speed class?

For video, speed class guarantees smooth recording. Bus speed determines how fast you can transfer files to your computer. Both matter but for different reasons.

What if my device supports UHS-III but my card is UHS-I?

The card will work but at UHS-I speeds. Cards are backward compatible, so an older UHS-I card works in a UHS-III device, just slower.
