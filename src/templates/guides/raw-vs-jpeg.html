RAW vs JPEG: Which Format Is Right for You?

Complete Photography Format Comparison

Learn the differences between RAW and JPEG formats, understand storage implications, and discover which format matches your photography style and workflow.

Understanding Image Formats

When you press the shutter button on your camera, you're capturing raw sensor data. Your camera then processes this data and saves it in a format: either RAW (unprocessed) or JPEG (processed and compressed).

Each format has distinct advantages and trade-offs regarding file size, storage, editing capability, and workflow. The choice depends on your photography style, post-processing needs, and available storage.

J JPEG (Joint Photographic Experts Group)

What Is JPEG?

JPEG is a processed, compressed image format. Your camera applies color science, white balance, saturation, sharpening, and other adjustments, then saves a final, ready-to-view image.

File Size & Storage

JPEG files are small due to compression. A 20MP JPEG is typically 5-8 MB. 1000 photos = 5-8 GB. Perfect for storage-conscious photographers.

Editing Capabilities

Limited. Once processed by the camera and saved as JPEG, you've lost the raw sensor data. Edits are destructiveâ€”adjusting exposure or white balance will degrade image quality.

Best For

  • Quick sharing to social media
  • Events where instant results are needed
  • Photographers who don't edit
  • Maximizing storage capacity
  • Travel and casual photography

Storage Tip: JPEG is ideal when storage is limited. You can capture many more JPEGs than RAW files on the same card.

R RAW (Unprocessed Sensor Data)

What Is RAW?

RAW is unprocessed, minimally compressed data directly from the camera sensor. It contains all the information captured, giving you complete control over processing and editing.

File Size & Storage

RAW files are large (30-50 MB for 20MP). 1000 photos = 30-50 GB. Requires higher-capacity SD cards and external storage for archiving.

Editing Capabilities

Extensive. RAW files contain the full tonal range and color data. You can adjust exposure, white balance, shadows, highlights, and color with non-destructive editing in software like Lightroom or Capture One.

Best For

  • Professional photographers
  • Editing-heavy workflows
  • Studio and portrait photography
  • Difficult lighting situations
  • Archival and future-proofing

Storage Tip: Plan for significant storage expansion. Many professionals use external SSDs or NAS systems to archive RAW files after shoots.

+ JPEG + RAW (Dual Format)

The Hybrid Approach

Many professional cameras can record both RAW and JPEG simultaneously. This gives you instant JPEGs for preview/delivery and RAW files for detailed editing.

File Size & Storage

Combined size: ~35-58 MB per photo. Requires larger cards and immediate offloading to external storage after shoots.

Best For

  • Wedding photographers (JPEGs for client previews, RAW for final editing)
  • Professional studio work
  • High-value shoots where no shots can be lost
  • Photographers who want immediate and post-processed versions

Storage Consideration: JPEG+RAW requires 256GB+ SD cards or multiple cards per shoot. Plan your storage strategy before starting.
