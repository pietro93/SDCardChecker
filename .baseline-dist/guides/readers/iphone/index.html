Don't snap your charging port. We tested the safest, fastest USB-C readers for offloading photos on the go.

Which iPhone Do You Have?

  • iPhone 15 / 16: Limited to USB 2.0 (60 MB/s). Don't buy expensive high-speed readers; you won't see the benefit.
  • iPhone 15 Pro / 16 Pro: Supports USB 3.0 (up to 280 MB/s real-world). You need a UHS-II reader to unlock these speeds.
  • iPhone 16 vs 15: Same USB-C connector, same speed tiers. Reader recommendations are identical for both generations.

Protect Your Port

Avoid rigid "Stick" dongles. If it plugs directly into the phone like a thumb drive, one accidental bump can snap your iPhone's USB-C port internally.

Always choose a reader with a flexible cable.

How to Edit RAW Photos on iPhone (Without Filling Storage)

  1. 1

    Don't "Import All"

    Transferring 500 RAW files (50MB each) eats 25GB of space instantly. Keep them on the SD card.

  2. 2

    Preview in "Files" App

    Your iPhone can preview RAW files directly from the card reader with zero lag. Swipe through and find your winners.

  3. 3

    Save Individual Shots

    Tap the Share icon on your best shot > "Save Image". This moves only that specific photo to your internal storage for editing in Lightroom Mobile.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does iPhone limit USB-C speeds?

iPhone 15 and iPhone 16 standard models use a bottleneck controller that limits USB to ~60 MB/s. iPhone 15 Pro and iPhone 16 Pro models use USB 3.0 for full-speed access (~280 MB/s real-world). It's a hardware limitation, not a reader issue — the reader recommendation is the same across both generations.

Can I charge my iPhone while using a reader?

Not simultaneously—iPhone only has one USB-C port. However, you can plug into power after transferring files. Most transfers complete in minutes, so it's rarely an issue.

Will a flexible cable reader damage my phone?

No. Flexible cables actually reduce stress on the port by absorbing physical impacts. This is why Apple's official reader uses a cable, not a rigid connector.

Why don't I recommend the Anker PowerExpand here?

Anker PowerExpand is a good reader, but it's a dual USB-C/USB-A dongle with a rigid design. For iPhone specifically, the cable-style readers (Apple, SanDisk, Transcend) are safer and better optimized.
